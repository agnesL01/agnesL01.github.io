Technical Courses Taken

The course introductions are from cornell class roster.

ECE2100: Introduction to Circuits for Electrical and Computer Engineers

This course is an introduction to electronic circuits. We build and analyze circuits comprising passive elements and active components.

ECE2200: Signals and Information

Introduction to signal processing. Topics include frequency-based representations: Fourier analysis and synthesis; discrete time linear systems: input/output relationships, filtering, spectral response; analog-to-digital and digital-to-analog conversion; continuous time signals and linear time invariant systems: frequency response and continuous-time Fourier transform.

ECE2300: Digital Logic and Computer Organization

This course provides an introduction to the design and implementation of digital circuits and microprocessors. Topics include transistor network design, Boolean algebra, combinational circuits, sequential circuits, finite state machine design, processor pipelines, and memory hierarchy.

ECE2400: Computer Systems Programming

Computer systems programming involves developing software to connect the low-level computer hardware to high-level, user-facing application software. This course will provide a strong foundation in the principles, practices, and art of computer systems programming using the C and C++ programming languages.

ECE3140: Embedded Systems

An introduction to the design of embedded systems, with an emphasis on understanding the interaction between hardware, software, and the physical world. Topics covered include assembly language programming, interrupts, I/O, concurrency management, scheduling, resource management, and real-time constraints.

ECE3150: Introduction to Microelectronics

Topics covered include basic semiconductor physics, PN junctions and diodes, photodetectors and solar cells, MOS capacitors, MOSFETs, BJTs, large signal and small signal models of electronic devices, single stage amplifiers, multistage amplifiers, differential amplifiers, analog circuit analysis and design, high-frequency models of devices, high-frequency circuit analysis, digital logic and MOS logic devices, complimentary MOS (or CMOS) logic gates, fundamental trade-offs in high speed analog and digital circuit design.

ECE3250: Mathematics of Signal and System Analysis

Topics include foundational material about sets and functions; modular arithmetic and public-key cryptography; inner products, orthogonal representations, and Fourier analysis; LTI systems as mappings on function spaces; sampling and interpolation; singular-value decomposition; and, as time permits, an introduction to wavelets and elementary convex analysis.

ECE3400: Intelligent Physical Systems

Students will engage in a microcontroller-based approach to building a robot which can perceive and act upon its environment. This course include topics on microcontrollers, sensors, motors & actuators, and circuit & software debugging.

ECE4320: Integrated Micro Sensors and Actuators: Bridging the Physical and Digital Worlds

Introduction to micro and nano devices that allow the digital world to both sense and actuate in the physical world. Design and analysis of modern MEMS/NEMS (Micro/Nano Electromechanical Systems) touch, accelerometers, gyroscopes, pressure, microphones, neural probe sensors. Design and analysis of electrostatic, piezoelectric, thermal, and magnetic actuators for frequency control and micro robotic applications.

ECE4740: Introduction to Digital (VLSI) Design

An introduction to digital very large-scale integration (VLSI) design from architectural considerations down to the layout level. Lecture, labs, and homework topics emphasize disciplined synchronous VLSI design and include topics of CMOS logic, layout, and timing; computer-aided design and analysis tools; electrical and performance considerations.

CS4780: Introduction to Machine Learning

The course provides an introduction to machine learning, focusing on supervised learning and its theoretical foundations. Topics include regularized linear models, boosting, kernels, deep networks, generative models, online learning, and ethical questions arising in ML applications.

ECE4320: Integrated Micro Sensors and Actuators: Bridging the Physical and Digital Worlds

Modern vertical farming systems require cost-effective, accurate sensing of environmental factors such as airflow, temperature, & humidity. In this paper, we fabricate, analyze, & simulate an integrated MEMS combined airflow, temperature, & humidity sensor for applications in vertical farming. We employ cantilever structures for the airflow and humidity sensors and thin-film RTD for the temperature sensor. Through COMSOL simulation, we achieve an airflow sensor sensitivity of .2 Î¼m / (m/s) and show that theoretical analysis comes within an order of magnitude of the simulated result. We further demonstrate reasonable isolation from temperature and humidity effects. Future work improving our sensor may enable individual plant-level monitoring for large scale vertical farming systems.

ECE4740: Introduction to Digital (VLSI) Design

In this final project, we initially constructed two 8-bit counters using two distinct types of D flip-flop implementations, namely static and dynamic. Subsequently, we compared the counters based on three factors: the minimum period required for the counter to function correctly with varying Vdd values, the overall area occupied by the counter, and the peak power consumption. Based on our analysis, we deduce that the dynamic flip flop proves to be a superior choice. Following that, we proceeded with the construction of our comprehensive N-bit counter with dynamic D flip-flop, which possesses the capability to select the desired bit count, ranging from 4 to 8.

ECE4530: Analog Integrated Circuit Design

Micro-sized cameras have great potential in identifying defects in the human body and enabling sensing for super-small robots. The performance, and resolution of micro-sized cameras is a challenge encountered in several engineering and medical applications. For instance, micro-sized camera images are fuzzy, and have limited fields of view. The objective of this project is to design a two-wires interface data camera. We aimed to optimize the power consumption of the design and reduce the complexity of the system by using a 2-wire interface. The designs consisted of 4 major blocks: rectifier, clock, array of 3-transistor pixels, and the connector interface between the pixels and the lines.
